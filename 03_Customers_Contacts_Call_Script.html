Why Customer Records Are the Foundation

In Rev.io PSA, a customer account is the anchor for everything. Every ticket gets linked to a customer. Every invoice gets sent to a customer. Every quote is built for a customer. Every asset you install lives under a customer. Every contract you manage is tied to a customer account.

Without clean, complete customer records, you'll have tickets floating without an owner, invoices going to the wrong place, and no way to pull a full history of work for a specific account. Getting customers in the system correctly from the start pays dividends across every other module.

→ Connects ToCustomer records are referenced in Ticketing (every ticket requires a customer), Billing (all invoices and payments attach to a customer), Quoting (quotes and proposals are built on a customer), Contracts, Recurring Services, Scheduling, and Assets.
2
Creating a Customer

Go to Customers > Add Customer. The key fields to get right:

  • Customer Name — how this account will appear across the entire system: on tickets, invoices, quotes, and dispatch schedules. Use the name you and your team know them by.
  • Account Number — assign manually or let the system auto-generate. Useful if you already use account numbers in your current system.
  • Customer Type — classifies the account. Common types: Residential, Commercial, Government, Non-Profit. These are used for filtering and reporting.
  • Stage — where the customer is in your relationship. Active, Prospect, Inactive. Only Active customers are available when creating tickets.
  • Address — the primary billing address. This appears on invoices. If the service location is different, that gets set at the ticket level.
❓ PromptLet's create a customer together — use a real account or a test record. Who do you want to start with?
3
Adding Contacts

Contacts are the individual people at the customer account — the person who calls in for support, receives invoices, approves quotes, or signs off on completed work. A customer account can have many contacts, but one should always be flagged as Primary.

From the customer record > Overview tab > Contacts sub-tab > Add Contact. Key fields: Name, Email (used for ticket notifications, portal access, and automatic customer matching), Phone, Role/Title, and the Primary Contact toggle.

❓ PromptAdd the primary contact for the customer we just created.
▲ Platform ImpactThe contact's email address is especially important — it's how the system matches inbound emails to the right customer account when email-to-ticket is configured, and it's where ticket notification emails are sent. An inaccurate email means your customers won't receive updates about their tickets.
4
The Customer Profile — A Quick Orientation

Now that we have a customer and contact, let me give you a quick tour of the customer profile tabs so you know where to find things:

  • Overview — contacts, portal users, account notes, and related accounts. Your first stop when you pull up a customer.
  • Service — all tickets, scheduled work, assets, contracts, and recurring services for this customer. The full service history lives here.
  • Billing — invoices, payment history, unposted charges, recurring services, and payment accounts. Everything financial for this account.
  • Quotes — opportunities and proposals. All quoting activity tied to this customer.

We'll go much deeper in each of these areas in later sessions. For now, just know where to look.

Action Items

  • Add your key customer accounts — start with your top 20–30
  • Add at least one primary contact per customer
  • Review Customer Types and Stages to make sure they match how you classify accounts
