1
Why the Product Catalog Drives Everything

The Product Catalog is the single source of truth for everything your company sells — parts, labor, and services. Every ticket charge your techs add, every line item on a quote, every recurring service charge, every package you sell — all of it references items in this catalog.

A clean, well-organized catalog means your team selects from a consistent list instead of typing free-form descriptions on every ticket. That consistency flows all the way through to billing accuracy, quote speed, and revenue reporting. A messy or incomplete catalog leads to inconsistent pricing, billing errors, and manual cleanup after the fact.

→ Connects ToThe Product Catalog feeds into Parts & Labor on tickets (billable charges), Quoting (line items on quotes and proposals), Packages (bundles of catalog items), Purchase Orders (items you order from vendors), Recurring Services (repeating charges), Contract Rates (what's covered or billed at a special rate), and Inventory (if tracking is enabled).
▲ Platform ImpactIf catalog items aren't set up before your team starts creating tickets and quotes, they'll enter charges as free-text — which breaks reporting, makes billing inconsistent, and is very hard to clean up retroactively.
2
Item Types

Before we start adding items, it's worth understanding the three types and when to use each:

  • Parts — physical items: equipment, hardware, materials. If you enable inventory tracking, parts will have quantities tracked across your warehouses. When a tech adds a part to a ticket, it decrements from your stock.
  • Labor — time-based services with an hourly rate. On-Site Labor, Remote Support, Travel Time. When a tech logs time on a ticket against a labor item, it can generate a billable charge automatically.
  • Services — flat-fee, non-inventory items. Managed service fees, project management charges, one-time setup fees. Neither tracked by quantity nor billed hourly — just a fixed price.
3
Creating an Item

Click Add Product. The key fields:

  • Name — what your team searches for when adding to a ticket or quote. Keep it clear and consistent.
  • Category — how items are grouped. Set up categories first; they make filtering and reporting much cleaner.
  • SKU / Part Number — your internal code or manufacturer part number. Useful for cross-referencing with a distributor or your accounting system.
  • Description — the customer-facing description that appears on invoices and quotes. Write it the way you want the customer to read it.
  • Unit Price and Cost — what you charge and what it costs you. Margin reporting uses both.
  • Tax Exempt toggle — mark this if the item should not be taxed on invoices.
  • Active toggle — only active items appear in searches. Deactivate to hide without deleting history.
❓ PromptLet's create your first item. What's something your team puts on almost every job?
💡 Internal NoteStart with a high-use labor rate or a frequently sold part. This lets them see the catalog in action immediately and validates that pricing is correct before they build out the rest.

Action Items

  • Build out item categories before bulk-adding items
  • Add all Parts, Labor types, and Services to the catalog
  • Verify pricing and cost on every item before go-live
  • Flag any items where tax-exempt status needs to be confirmed
