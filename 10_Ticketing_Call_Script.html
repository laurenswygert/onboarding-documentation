Tickets — The Engine of the Platform

Tickets are the operational heartbeat of Rev.io PSA. Every service request, every job, every customer interaction runs through a ticket. They're where work gets assigned, time gets logged, parts get added, customers get updated, and billing gets triggered. Almost everything else we've configured — user groups, ticket defaults, the product catalog, customers and contacts, assets, contracts — either feeds into tickets or gets triggered by them.

Today we're going to walk the full ticket lifecycle: from creation through every tab, through the workflow, all the way to close. By the end you'll know exactly what happens on a ticket at every stage.

→ Connects ToTickets pull from: Customer records (linked customer and contact), User Groups and Users (assignment), Product Catalog (Parts & Labor tab charges), Contracts (determines billing rates), Assets (Items Serviced tab), Purchase Orders (ordered for this job), and System Settings (default status, type, priority, and user group). When a ticket closes, its charges flow to Billing.
2
Creating a Ticket

From the Service module, click Create Ticket. Walk through the key fields:

  • Customer — links to the customer account. Pulling in their contact history and contracts.
  • Contact — the specific person at that company associated with this request.
  • Subject — a clear, consistent description. This is what shows in every list view, so make it meaningful.
  • Ticket Type — the category of work. Break/Fix, Installation, Maintenance, Project. Pre-populated from the catalog you configured in System Settings.
  • Status — where the ticket is in your workflow. Starts at your configured default.
  • Priority — urgency level. Starts at your default and can be changed.
  • Assigned User / User Group — who is responsible. You can assign to an individual, a group, or both.
  • Description — the full detail of the request. Internal — your team sees this.

3
Inside a Ticket — Tab by Tab

Once a ticket is created, a set of tabs across the top organizes all the work that happens on that ticket. Let's walk through each one:

  • Activity — the communication log. Internal notes (visible to your team only) and public notes (visible to the customer and emailed out if that setting is on). Signatures are captured here too.
  • Parts & Labor — the billable charges. Select items from your catalog — parts used, labor performed, services rendered. What you add here flows directly into billing when the ticket is sent to invoice.
  • Time Logs — where your team tracks hours spent. Use the built-in timer or enter manually. Mark as billable or non-billable. Time logs feed into both billing and timesheet reports.
  • Tasks — checklist items for multi-step jobs. Assign to a user, mark complete. Great for ensuring nothing gets skipped on a complex job.
  • Purchase Orders — if you need to order parts for this specific job, you create the PO here. It stays linked so anyone can see what was ordered and whether it arrived.
  • Addresses — the service location for this job. May differ from the customer's billing address. Important for field dispatch and mapping.
  • Items Serviced — link Assets to this ticket. Every time you work on a specific piece of equipment and log it here, you're building that equipment's service history.
▲ Platform ImpactThe Parts & Labor tab is critical for billing accuracy. If techs add charges directly to the catalog items you've set up, pricing is consistent and billing is fast. If they free-type charges, you lose that consistency and have to manually clean it up before invoicing.

Ticket Workflow

Tickets move through statuses as work progresses, using the Stage panel on the right side of the ticket. Your team should be updating status in real time — it keeps your queue accurate and your reports meaningful.

❓ PromptWalk me through how a typical ticket moves through your business — from the moment it comes in to when you close it. What are the stages?
💡 Internal NoteListen carefully here. If their workflow doesn't map cleanly to the statuses configured in System Settings, note it as a follow-up — they may need to adjust the status list.

Email-to-Ticket: When a customer emails your support address, PSA can automatically convert that email into a ticket — matching it to the right customer by email domain, creating the ticket, and dropping the original email as the first activity note. This eliminates manual ticket creation for inbound requests. Configured under Admin > Email Settings.

❓ PromptDo you have a dedicated support email address that customers currently use to submit requests?
→ Connects ToClosing a ticket triggers the billing workflow — charges from the Parts & Labor tab and Time Logs become unposted charges on the customer's billing account, ready to be invoiced. This is the bridge between service delivery and revenue collection.

Action Items

  • Create a handful of real tickets to practice the workflow end-to-end
  • Have techs start logging time on tickets from day one — this is critical for billing accuracy
  • Set up email-to-ticket if you have a support mailbox (coordinate with your PM)
  • Review your ticket statuses and confirm the flow matches how your team actually works
