1
Why the Catalog Is Everything

The Product Catalog is the single source of truth for all billable items in Rev.io PSA. Every charge on a ticket, every line item on a quote, every item in a package, every recurring service charge, every contract rate — all of it references a catalog item. The catalog is the foundation your revenue depends on.

A well-structured catalog produces consistent pricing, accurate billing, clean reports, and fast quoting. A poorly structured catalog — with duplicate items, inconsistent naming, missing prices, or wrong tax settings — produces billing errors, pricing inconsistency, and reporting that can't be trusted.

→ Connects ToThe Product Catalog feeds: Parts & Labor on Tickets (billable charges), Quoting (line items on quotes and proposals), Packages (component items), Purchase Orders (items you order from vendors), Recurring Services (the catalog item defines the charge description and price), Contract Rates (discounted or included rates reference catalog items), and Inventory (Parts with tracking enabled have quantities tracked).
▲ Platform ImpactIf techs can't find what they need in the catalog, they'll type free-form descriptions and prices on tickets. Those free-form entries don't tie to any catalog item, can't be reported on by product, and create billing inconsistency. The catalog investment pays for itself in the first month of live operation.

❓ PromptBefore we start building, let's understand your mix. What percentage of your revenue comes from labor/time, from parts, and from flat-fee services? That helps us prioritize what to build first.

4
Catalog Organization Strategy

Before adding items, plan your taxonomy. A well-organized catalog is self-maintaining; a disorganized one grows into an unusable mess over time.

Naming Conventions

  • Be specific: 'On-Site Labor — Standard Rate' instead of 'Labor'
  • Be consistent: Use the same format for similar items. 'Travel Time — Standard' and 'Travel Time — After Hours' not 'Travel' and 'After Hours Travel'
  • Think searchability: Techs search this field in the field. If they call it 'on-site,' don't name it 'field labor'
  • Version or tier in the name: 'Managed Service — Tier 1', 'Managed Service — Tier 2' instead of creating ambiguity

Category Architecture

  • Create top-level categories that match your major service lines or billing categories
  • Sub-categories are optional but useful for catalogs with 100+ items
  • Every item should have a category — uncategorized items disappear in reporting
  • Categories match what you want to see on revenue-by-product reports
💡 Pro TipRun a 'catalog audit' before go-live: sort the full catalog by category and scroll through it as if you were a tech looking for something. If you can't find items quickly, reorganize before your team starts using the system.
5
Labor Rates — Special Considerations

Labor items are the most frequently used catalog items for service businesses. Getting them right has an outsized impact on billing accuracy and tech efficiency.

Common Labor Rate Structures

ScenarioRecommended Setup
Single rate for all workOne 'Standard Labor' item. Simple, easy.
Different rates by typeOn-Site Labor, Remote Labor, Travel Time as separate items. Allows billing segmentation.
After-hours premiumStandard Labor + After-Hours Labor as separate items, or a surcharge add-on item.
Multiple techs at different ratesOne item per rate tier, or use per-user rates configured on the user record (overrides catalog rate).
Project vs. break/fix different ratesSeparate items for each context. Allows revenue reporting by work type.

Assign a Default Labor Rate on each technician's user record — the catalog labor item that auto-populates when they log time on a ticket. This eliminates the need for techs to select their rate every time they log time.

6
Pricing Strategy and Margin Tracking

Every catalog item has a Unit Price (what you charge) and a Cost (what it costs you). The margin between these two values is tracked per item and surfaced in Analytics reporting.

For the catalog to be useful for profitability analysis, Cost must be accurate. For Parts, cost is the purchase price from your vendor. For Labor, cost is the technician's hourly wage or fully-loaded cost rate. For Services, cost is whatever internal overhead you associate with delivering it.

❓ PromptDo you want to track margins at the item level? If so, we need cost on every item, not just price. Do you have cost data available for your labor rates and key parts?
⚠ Watch OutDo not leave Cost blank if you plan to run profitability reports. A missing cost is treated as zero, which makes your margin calculations appear artificially high.

Action Items

  • Define and create all catalog categories before adding items
  • Establish a naming convention and document it for your team
  • Build all Labor rate items first — these are used on every ticket
  • Add all Services (flat-fee items) next
  • Build the Parts catalog, starting with highest-volume items
  • Enter both Unit Price AND Cost on every item
  • Set tax-exempt status correctly on every item
  • Enable inventory tracking only on physical Parts
  • Assign default labor rates to all technician user records
  • Review the full catalog before go-live — sort by category and verify
